Downscaling

Reanalyses capture only the large-scale, but some variables vary strongly on much smaller scales. This small-scale variation depends on topography or land cover (e.g., near coast lines), for instance. Reanalysis data therefore often require spatial refinement. The same is true for weather forecasts, or for climate scenarios. The corresponding approaches are called “downscaling”.

Two classes of approaches are distinguished: numerical and statistical downscaling. The former requires a global model or reanalysis, into which a regional numerical weather prediction model is embedded. This nesting can be repeated to obtain local information. The regional model adds information on topography and land cover, while meteorological information is passed on from large to small scales. Weather forecasts are done in that way. Statistical downscaling uses statistical relations between the large-scale predictors (e.g., from a reanalysis) and local weather variables from observations.

An intuitive statistical method is analog downscaling, which assumes that similar large-scale conditions lead to similar local weather. If a sufficiently large set of conditions is available for both, large-scale variables and local weather, then local weather can be estimated for a given large-scale situation by choosing the most similar case.
